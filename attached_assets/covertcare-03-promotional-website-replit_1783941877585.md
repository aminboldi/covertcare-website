## One-sentence description

## Short description

In some high-risk and disrupted settings, people can reach a trusted community intermediary but not the right clinician. CovertCare proposes a low-bandwidth, minimal-data workflow for structured intake, qualified review, clear disposition, and referral—operated under a legitimate health or humanitarian partner.

## Long description

CovertCare is a public-interest digital-health initiative in pilot design. It addresses a narrow problem: how trained field intermediaries can obtain qualified clinical guidance without spreading unnecessary identity, location, and case information across informal communication channels.

The proposed workflow is partner-operated and clinician-governed. A host organization would enroll participants, define the clinical scope, verify professional roles, establish referral pathways, determine appropriate record retention, and manage incidents. CovertCare would provide the minimum-data intake, routing, disposition, follow-up, training, and implementation layer.

The project does not promise anonymity, act as an emergency service, replace physical examination, provide AI diagnosis, or operate a public doctor marketplace. Its first objective is to test whether one bounded pilot can improve response timeliness and referral clarity while reducing unnecessary personal-data collection.

## Objections and responses

### Objection: “This sounds like secure messaging.”

**Response:** Secure transport is only one layer. CovertCare focuses on minimum clinical data, verified roles, appropriate routing, governed disposition, referral closure, retention, and organizational responsibility.

### Objection: “A centralized host creates risk.”

**Response:** Any architecture creates risk. The pilot will compare compartmentalized, federated, and hosted options under a specific threat model. Decentralization is not assumed to be safer by default.

### Objection: “A record conflicts with privacy.”

**Response:** Clinical accountability may require a minimum record. The project aims to retain the least information required under the host’s legal and clinical obligations, then apply a documented deletion policy.

### Objection: “Why not begin in the highest-need conflict?”

**Response:** The first pilot should validate the workflow where clinical governance, referral, and supervised learning are possible. Deploying prematurely in the hardest threat environment would be irresponsible.

### Objection: “Can the system prove the patient is real?”

**Response:** Cryptography cannot independently prove a real-world medical fact without a trusted attestation source. Clinical assessment remains human judgement under uncertainty.

### Objection: “Is the project available today?”

**Response:** No. CovertCare is in concept and pilot-design stage. The website is recruiting appropriate partners and advisors, not accepting medical cases.

## Section 5 — Key Insight

### Eyebrow

**The design principle**

### Headline

**Privacy is a care-pathway decision—not a promise of invisibility**

### Supporting body

CovertCare will not claim “no trace,” “no metadata,” or perfect anonymity. Devices, networks, counterparties, software dependencies, and human behaviour can create residual risk. The proposed model begins with a more defensible commitment: collect less, define responsibility, use established security components, and make limitations visible.

### Four principles

#### Minimum necessary data

Do not request a name, government ID, or precise location unless the approved care pathway truly requires it.

#### Known roles, limited disclosure

The host organization verifies intermediaries and clinicians and grants only the access required for their role.

#### Clear clinical disposition

Each case should end with an approved next step, a request for more information, an escalation, or an explicit statement that it is outside scope.

#### Threat-modelled deployment

Every implementation must define adversaries, assets, device assumptions, network exposure, retention, incident response, and residual risk.

## Section 7 — How It Works

### Eyebrow

**Illustrative pilot workflow**

### Headline

**Minimum data in. Clearer next step out.**

### Step 1 — Partner-controlled access

The host organization approves trained intermediaries and qualified clinicians under its own clinical and legal governance.

### Step 2 — Minimal structured case

The intermediary obtains consent and submits only approved clinical information, such as age band, symptom category, duration, red flags, relevant observations, and broad access constraints.

### Step 3 — Appropriate routing

A coordinator or approved rule sends the case to a suitable clinician during a defined service window.

### Step 4 — Governed disposition

The clinician requests more information, provides approved self-care information, recommends routine or urgent in-person assessment, escalates an emergency, requests specialist review, or marks the case outside scope.

### Step 5 — Referral and closure

The intermediary communicates the next step and records whether referral was understood or completed using the least data required.

## Section 8 — Illustrative Example

### Eyebrow

**A hypothetical scenario**

### Headline

**From a fragmented request to a governed next step**

### Before

A community health worker receives a request from an adult with several days of worsening symptoms and difficulty reaching a clinic. The worker asks personal contacts, repeats the story across messages, waits for replies, and receives no consistent referral status.

### Proposed pilot flow

The worker obtains consent, creates a case code, completes a minimum-data form, receives a qualified disposition during staffed hours, shares the recommended next step, and records whether a referral option was used.

### What would be measured

- time to qualified response;

## Section 15 — FAQ

### FAQ 1 — Does CovertCare provide medical help today?

No. CovertCare is in concept and pilot-design stage. This website does not accept medical cases or connect visitors with clinicians.

### FAQ 2 — Is CovertCare an emergency service?

No. It does not provide emergency response, diagnosis, treatment, prescribing, or guaranteed availability.

### FAQ 3 — Is the proposed system anonymous?

No system can guarantee anonymity. The proposed model aims to minimize unnecessary identifying data and reduce specific risks under a documented threat model.

### FAQ 4 — Why not use Signal?

Signal provides mature end-to-end encrypted messaging and deliberately reduces metadata. CovertCare’s proposed value is the clinical and institutional workflow around communication: minimum-data intake, approved roles, routing, disposition, referral, and follow-up.

### FAQ 5 — Does CovertCare use zero-knowledge proofs?

Not in the first pilot. Privacy-preserving credentials may later help prove a clinician role or authorization without revealing unnecessary attributes. They cannot independently prove that a person has a real-world medical condition.

### FAQ 6 — Is the system decentralized?

No architecture has been selected as an ideology. The pilot will use the least complex supportable architecture and evaluate compartmentalized or federated options against a real threat model.

### FAQ 7 — Who can become a pilot partner?

A legitimate health or humanitarian organization with a real care-coordination problem, a clinical owner, trained intermediaries, a referral pathway, and the ability to participate in legal, safety, and security review.

### FAQ 8 — Can an individual volunteer doctor join?

The public website may collect advisor interest, but it must not enroll clinicians into a care network. Clinical participation would occur only through an approved host and credentialing process.

### FAQ 9 — Will the code be open source?

The recommended model includes an open-source core, subject to governance, security, operational-safety, licensing, and maintenance decisions. Do not describe code as open source until a real repository and license exist.

### FAQ 10 — How will impact be measured?

Through response timeliness, clinical completeness, data minimization, referral closure, safety incidents, provider burden, cost, and the host’s continuation decision. Health outcomes require longer and more rigorous evaluation.

### FAQ 11 — Why the name CovertCare?

It is a working name intended to signal privacy-conscious access. Because “covert” may sound militarized or intelligence-oriented, the team will test the name with humanitarian, clinical, and community partners before public deployment.

### FAQ 12 — What information can I submit through the website?

Only non-sensitive institutional or professional inquiry information. Do not submit patient details, symptoms, medical records, injury images, incident evidence, precise locations, political information, or urgent requests.

Avoid the metaphor of invisibility, hiding, disappearance, or defeating an adversary.
